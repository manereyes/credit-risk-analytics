# Credit Score Module

## Credit Risk Analytics Suite

---

## 1. Module Overview

The `credit_score.py` module is responsible for transforming the raw financial and credit characteristics of a customer into an educational **Credit Score**.

The module is the second stage of the Credit Risk Analytics Suite pipeline.

The general flow of the project is:

```text
Customer Generator
        │
        ▼
Customer DataFrame
        │
        ▼
┌──────────────────────┐
│   Credit Score       │
│      Module          │
└──────────────────────┘
        │
        ▼
Raw Credit Score
        │
        ▼
Standardized Credit Score
        │
        ▼
Credit Rating
        │
        ▼
PD Model
```

The objective of this module is to simulate the type of logic that a financial institution could use to transform several credit-related characteristics into a single numerical score.

This score is then used as one of the inputs for later stages of the credit decision process.

The module does **not** determine whether a customer defaults.

That responsibility belongs to the Probability of Default module.

Instead, this module answers the following question:

> **How strong is the customer's current credit profile according to the institution's scoring policy?**

---

# 2. Business Context

When a financial institution evaluates a customer, it usually needs to summarize many different characteristics into an interpretable measure of credit quality.

A customer may have:

* A history of late payments.
* High or low credit utilization.
* A short or long credit history.
* A diversified or limited credit portfolio.
* Many or few recent credit inquiries.

These variables have different meanings and scales.

For example:

```text
Late Payments
0 – 5

Credit Utilization
0% – 100%

Credit History
1 – 20 years

Credit Mix
1 – 4 products

Recent Inquiries
0 – 5
```

It would be difficult to combine these variables directly.

For example:

```text
Late Payments = 2
Credit Utilization = 70%
Credit History = 8 years
Credit Mix = 3
Recent Inquiries = 1
```

The numerical values are not directly comparable.

A value of `70` for Credit Utilization does not mean that utilization should automatically have more influence than a value of `2` for Late Payments.

Therefore, the module first converts each raw characteristic into a standardized **sub-score**.

The process becomes:

```text
Raw Customer Variable
        │
        ▼
Individual Score
        │
        ▼
Weighted Score
        │
        ▼
Raw Credit Score
        │
        ▼
Credit Score
        │
        ▼
Credit Rating
```

This creates a simple educational scoring system.

The module evaluates five dimensions.

```text
3 late payments
        ↓
Moderate score

4+ late payments

Poor score
```

The function is:

```python
def payment_history_score(late_payments: int) -> int:
```

The function receives:


For example:

```text
Late Payments    Score

2                 75
3                 55
4+                20
```

The idea is simple:

> More late payments indicate a higher level of historical credit risk.

# 6. Credit Utilization Score

Credit utilization measures how much of the customer's available revolving credit is currently being used.

For example:

Suppose a customer has:

```text
Credit Limit = $10,000
Current Balance = $3,000
```

Then:

```text
Credit Utilization = 30%
```

A lower utilization is generally interpreted as a healthier credit profile.

The scoring logic is:

```text
Utilization < 30%
        ↓
Score = 100

30% – 49%
        ↓
Score = 80

50% – 69%
        ↓
Score = 60

70%+
        ↓
Score = 25
```

The function is:

```python
def utilization_score(utilization: float) -> int:
```

The input is the utilization percentage.

The output is a standardized score.

For example:

```python
utilization_score(25)
```

returns:

```text
100
```

While:

```python
utilization_score(75)
```

returns:

```text
25
```

The business interpretation is:

> Customers using a large proportion of their available credit are considered more financially constrained.

The scoring logic is:

```text
> 10 years
        ↓

        ↓
Score = 80

2 – 4 years
        ↓
Score = 60

< 2 years
        ↓
Score = 30
```

The function is:

```python
def credit_history_score(years: float) -> int:
```

The key concept is:

> A longer credit history does not automatically mean the customer is safer, but it gives the institution more behavioral information.

This is why the variable contributes positively to the score.

# 10. Individual Scoring Functions

One of the most important design decisions in this module is that each scoring dimension has its own function.

For example:

```python
payment_history_score()
```

handles payment history.

```python
utilization_score()
```

handles credit utilization.

```python
credit_history_score()
```

handles credit history.

```python
credit_mix_score()
```

handles credit mix.

```python
recent_activity_score()
```

handles recent inquiries.

This approach is called **modularization**.

Instead of creating one large function containing every business rule, we separate the logic into smaller components.

The architecture is:

```text
                 Customer
                    │
        ┌───────────┼───────────┐
        │           │           │
        ▼           ▼           ▼
Payment         Utilization   History
Score           Score         Score
        │           │           │
        └───────────┼───────────┘
                    │
        ┌───────────┼───────────┐
        │           │           │
        ▼           ▼           ▼
      Mix        Activity     ...
        │           │
        └───────────┼───────────┘
                    ▼
             Weighted Average
                    │
                    ▼
              Raw Score
```

This has several advantages.

If the institution changes the utilization policy, we only need to modify:

```python
utilization_score()
```

The rest of the system remains unchanged.

This is an example of **separation of concerns**.

Each function has one primary responsibility.

# 11. Raw Credit Score

After calculating the five individual scores, the module combines them into a single Raw Credit Score.

The model uses the following weights:

| Dimension          | Weight |
| ------------------ | -----: |
| Payment History    |    35% |
| Credit Utilization |    30% |
| Credit History     |    15% |
| Credit Mix         |    10% |
| Recent Activity    |    10% |

The weights sum to:

$
0.35 + 0.30 + 0.15 + 0.10 + 0.10 = 1.00
$

Therefore, the Raw Credit Score is calculated as a weighted average.

The mathematical representation is:

$
RawScore =
0.35(PaymentHistory)
+
0.30(Utilization)
+
0.15(CreditHistory)
+
0.10(CreditMix)
+
0.10(RecentActivity)
$

Each component is already between approximately `0` and `100`.

Therefore, the final Raw Score also remains approximately between `0` and `100`.

For example, suppose a customer receives:

```text
Payment History = 90
Utilization = 80
Credit History = 80
Credit Mix = 60
Recent Activity = 90
```

The calculation becomes:

$
RawScore =
(90 \times 0.35)
+
(80 \times 0.30)
+
(80 \times 0.15)
+
(60 \times 0.10)
+
(90 \times 0.10)
$

The result is:

[
RawScore = 81.5
]

The function responsible for this calculation is:

```python
def calculate_raw_score(customer: pd.Series) -> int:
```

The function receives one customer.

It then calls the five scoring functions:

```python
payment_history_score()
utilization_score()
credit_history_score()
credit_mix_score()
recent_activity_score()
```

The results are then combined using the predefined weights.

This function acts as the **aggregator** of the individual scoring components.

---

# 12. Why Use a Weighted Score?

Not every variable should have the same importance.

Consider the following:

```text
Payment History
35%

Credit Utilization
30%
```

These two dimensions represent:

```text
65% of the total score
```

This means the model considers actual payment behavior and current credit usage more important than credit mix or recent inquiries.

This reflects a basic credit-risk principle:

> Historical repayment behavior and current financial pressure are highly relevant when evaluating credit quality.

The weighting system is therefore a form of **business policy encoded into mathematics**.

The model is not simply calculating an average.

It is expressing the institution's opinion about which characteristics matter more.

---

# 13. Raw Score vs Credit Score

The Raw Score is not the final Credit Score.

The Raw Score is an internal score between approximately:

```text
0 – 100
```

The final Credit Score is transformed into a more familiar range:

```text
400 – 850
```

The transformation is:

$
CreditScore = 400 + (4.5 \times RawScore)
$

The function responsible for this transformation is:

```python
def normalize_credit_score(raw_score: float) -> int:
```

For example:

If:

$
RawScore = 80
$

Then:

$
CreditScore = 400 + (4.5 \times 80)
$

$
CreditScore = 760
$

Therefore:

```text
Raw Score
80
  │
  ▼
Normalization
  │
  ▼
Credit Score
760
```

The purpose of this transformation is mainly interpretability.

Instead of exposing the internal weighted score, the application presents a standardized score that is easier to understand.

Each scoring dimension has its own function.

The module focuses exclusively on Credit Score calculation.

It does not:

* Calculate PD.
* Approve loans.

The scoring functions can be reused for individual customers.

The business rules are contained inside dedicated functions.

# 21. Important Distinction: Credit Score vs Probability of Default

The Credit Score and Probability of Default are related, but they are not the same thing.

The Credit Score answers:

> How strong is the customer's credit profile according to the scoring policy?

The Probability of Default answers:

> How likely is the customer to default according to the PD model?

Conceptually:

```text
Credit Profile
      │
      ├───────────────┐
      │               │
      ▼               ▼
Credit Score          PD Model
      │               │
      ▼               ▼
Credit Quality       Default Risk
```

A customer can have:

```text
High Credit Score
+
Low PD
```

which represents a strong profile.

Another customer might have:

```text
Low Credit Score
+
High PD
```

which represents a risky profile.

These two measures will later work together in the Credit Decision Engine.

# 23. Summary

The `credit_score.py` module converts several raw customer characteristics into a structured Credit Score.

The process consists of three main stages:

```text
1. Calculate individual scores
           ↓
2. Combine them using weighted averages
           ↓
```

The module therefore creates:

```text

Credit Score
```

Instead:

```text

        +

        +
Credit History
        +
Credit Mix
        +
Recent Credit Activity

Weighted Credit Assessment
        ↓
Credit Score
```

This score will become one of the main inputs for the next stage of the project.
